Is the Christian God really omnipotent?

18 Feb 2024

Omnipotence, as well as the related properties of omniscience and omnipresence, is part of the Christian doctrine. However, is God really omnipotent, according to the Bible?

Reading the Bible, I incline to think He isn’t. There’s no doubt His powers, according to the Bible, are great – but I don’t think He is technically omnipotent. The extent of God’s powers is discussed in various parts of the Bible, but they all seem to be in accordance to (Psalm 115:3):

3Our God is in the heavens;
He does all that He pleases.

We are told that God can do anything He pleases – anything he desires, or wishes. This is often interpreted as God being omnipotent, but it is not so.

Certainly, an omnipotent being would be able to accomplish anything he desires. But omnipotence is not necessary to achieve this.

Consider the statement “Mr Jones can buy whatever he pleases”. Does that mean Mr Jones has unlimited money? No, it simply means that if Mr Jones desires something, he can buy it. If he can’t buy something, he doesn't desire it.

Similarly, God can do whatever He pleases. This doesn't mean He can do anything. There might be things He cannot do; but in this case, He doesn't please to do them.

Stating that God is omnipotent based on this statement from the Bible is simply overreaching: it is assigning attributes to God that are not deduced from it.

This literal interpretation of (Psalm 115:3), and others similar to it, also solves the logical paradox of whether God can create an immovable rock. At first glance, assuming God is omnipotent, this is a paradox: if He wasn't able to, He wouldn't be omnipotent; if He is omnipotent, He should be able to create it. But God should be able to move it too – meaning it is not really an immovable rock.

In view of the given interpretation of (Psalm 115:3), we see this needs not be a paradox. God can do anything He pleases. If He cannot create an immovable rock, it is because He doesn't please to. If he can create one, then He simply doesn't please to move it. There is no paradox here.
