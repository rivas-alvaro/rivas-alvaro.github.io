Punishing an illicit activity, or the activity that facilitates it?

16 Aug 2023

I was reading Hot Money, by Naomi Klein. There, the author advocates for stopping (or greatly reducing) pollution, which she (rightly, I believe) considers to be illicit. As part of the condemnation of pollution, she charges against fossil fuel-producing companies, advocating for punishing them. This, I believe, is a mistake, as whereas polluting is illicit activity, the business of these companies is not.

As far as I know, the business of fossil fuel-producing companies consists of extracting the fossil fuel, possibly producing derivative products out of it (eg gasoline out of oil) and selling it. They don’t really pollute, as far I can tell – not, at least, beyond the typical business of its size. The extraction of fossil fuels might incur pollution, but the bulk of the overall pollution produced by the extracted fuel is not due to actions by the fossil fuel-producing company. The polluters are the buyers of these products. They are the ones releasing carbon to the atmosphere. They are the ones that should be punished. The fossil fuel-producing companies are not the ones committing the illicit act.

The same applies to car manufacturers. Even though their product (assuming it’s not an electric car) does pollute when used, the act of producing and selling is a licit one. It is the end users – the drivers – the ones that should be punished.

If you are willing to punish fossil fuel-producers because they facilitate the illicit act that is polluting, you are running into a slippery slope. If an engineering company supplies the oil-extracting machinery (specialised machinery only used for oil extraction) they are too facilitating the polluting, so they would also need to be punished. The same would apply to the steel manufacturing company that provided the steel to the engineering company – and so on.

We should keep track of strict carbon accounting. However, the problem with how carbon accounting (assigning the amount of carbon emitted to an individual) is often done is double-counting. The same molecule of carbon is assigned to multiple parties.

Say I buy an avocado that was grown in California. The avocado had to be grown and transported to the UK. Transatlantic transportation pollutes a lot. How much carbon should be attributed to me, in this carbon accounting?

People tend to attribute the carbon emitted during transportation to me. But they also attribute it to the farmer, the transport company and the British grocery store. In other words, the same molecule of carbon was added to the carbon books of four parties. That is bad accounting.

The reality is simple. A molecule of carbon should be added to the emitter’s book – and no one else’s. I didn’t emit any carbon when buying the avocado – the transport company did. If we see polluting as illicit or immoral, then I did nothing wrong – the transport company is in the wrong.

Each molecule of carbon emitted to the atmosphere has an emitter. That person is responsible for this illicit act. Third parties that might have facilitated this polluting but haven’t themselves polluted are not acting unjustly.
